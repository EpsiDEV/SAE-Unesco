The Great Flowerbed

The creation of the Great Flowerbed between 1660 and 1664 - the largest in Europe, with its 14 hectares - by André Le Nôtre and Louis Le Vau, testifies to the Louis XIV's desire to clarify space at Fontainebleau.

The Park

Extending beyond the slope of the Bassin des Cascades, the park once marked the boundaries of the royal estate to the east. From the village Avon, it was crossed by the main access road to the château. Its current configuration, the organization of the network of cascatelles and is the result of the creation, under Henri IV, of a large canal nearly 1,200 meters long (1606-1609) and 40 meters wide. Initially planted with over sixty thousand trees, including rows of rows of white poplars, oaks and fruit trees, this "closed park park" and its canal were the pride of the king, who supervised the construction. In 1609, it took more than a week to fill the canal, and by autumn and by autumn, the king was sailing through it.
